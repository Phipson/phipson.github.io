Timeline

June 2018 - September 2018

Project Content

Unity Development (C#); Mobile AR/VR Programming; User Study; Gesture Design; Network Interface

Commercial CAD Software offer limited opportunities for end users to design products collaboratively. To design 3D objects using a 2D user interface, users typically have to surmount a steep learning curve, and then design using nonintuitive modes of input dissimilar to conventional design interactions involving pencil and paper. Additionally, commercial CAD Software offer little physical reference points: to prototype and evaluate the functionality of their models, users would have to manufacture their designs, perform tests, and modify their designs thereafter. This poses a bottleneck in the CADCAM workflow, especially in furniture design, where designers have to work with clients to create aesthetically pleasing, functional, and ergonomically friendly products.

Advancements in VR and mobile AR addresses some of these challenges, by utilizing expressive modes of input such as 3D hand gestures, and introducing 3D virtual environments scaled to real world dimensions. To overcome the challenges of collaborative CAD, we introduce a Collaborative Augmented and Virtual Environment for Computer-Aided Design (CAVECAD), which is a CAD tool that offers a collaborative design environment in VR and mobile AR, for end users to design fabricable flat-pack furniture collaboratively or independently, in the same or remote environments, simultaneously or at different points in time.

For this research project, I also drafted a project proposal, which examines existing research and literature around CAD in AR and VR.

My drafted research paper and video can also be found on this page. Both materials were submitted for ACM SIGCHI 2019.

Research Process

User Profiles

To motivate the need for 3D collaborative CAD tools in furniture design, I brainstormed and introduced several user profiles who would use or benefit from collaborative CAD tools in AR and VR.

Edith

Edith is a professional designer new to CAD but with prior experience designing with pencil and paper. As part of team project, she wants to send designs to her colleagues:

1. To get design feedback, Edith must upload file onto a cloud or communicate through email. Ideally, she would like to receive feedback as she designs, to maximize her design productivity.

2. Edith would like to bring her existing design experiences into a computer-based environment. However, because she is unfamiliar with keyboard and mouse interfaces, she has to learn from scratch.

Carl

Carl is a client who is working remotely with expert designers to create a customized table for his new house. He wants to offer feedback and suggestions, but finds it difficult to convey his needs through call

1. He would want the designer to see the physical layout of his house and understand the aesthetics of his scene when designing the furniture.

2. Upon designing the table, Carl may want to reconfigure his house. As a result, he may want the designer to also give him some suggestions and advice, but communicating through call is ineffective.

Kathy

Kathy is a designer who is working with a group of manufacturers to prototype and create furniture.

1. Before manufacturing her prototype, Kathy would like to get a preview of her design in the physical space, to evaluate the design aesthetics.

2. She would like to ask manufacturers for their opinion on her design, to see whether her ideas are realistic and whether there may be issues. Hence, she needs to communicate the measurements and physical constraints of her design.

Jason

Jason is designing a new office but would like external feedback and support from his team. However, due to time zone differences, it is impossible to meet and discuss project at the same time.

1. He needs mechanism of showcasing content and commenting on projects at different points in time.

2. He would like his team to evaluate the user experience of the office space, and whether the table and cubicle allocations are suitable. However, sending videos and images may not convey the same experience.

Creating a Design API

As part of our project, we collaborated with the UCLA Lab for Embedded Machines and Ubiquitous Robots and used their backend system to procedurally create fabricable flat-pack furniture. In order to interact with their backend, I designed and created a customized Unity API that connected to the backend server, and, given a set of parameters, generated a desired furniture as supported by the system. The resulting furniture was sent back through the network into our Unity server, parsed as an STL file, which was rendered and displayed in our game engine.

To ensure that the interface would work with either AR or VR environment, I also worked with members in my research team to generalize our API such that it was hardware independent. To further conceal the underlying complexity of the system, we further packaged the system as a set of scripts, so users could easily generate, remove, and modify their furnitures using simple function calls.

Code for the API can be found in this Github repository.

Integrating API into AR and VR

With two Nokia smartphones and an HTC Vive available, I separated the project into two separate concurrent sub-tasks. The first task involved integrating the API into the VR environment using the HTC Vive. The second involved doing so in the AR environment, using the Nokia smartphones. Both environments were designed on Unity, to ensure that the code could be used and targeted for devices outside of our existing VR and AR systems, and to streamline the code with the API.

To interact with our API in respective 3D environments, we defined a unified gesture library that allowed users to interact uniformly with the furniture using both a 2D touchscreen (as with mobile AR), and 3D hand gestures (as with VR). For 3D hand gestures, we used the Hi5 VR Gloves to track hand poses and gestures. Whereas my colleagues were responsible for the VR interface, I was responsible for the mobile AR API.

To ensure a streamlined method of interacting and collaborating between AR and VR environments, we defined a unified gesture library that would utilize the different modes of input in both AR and VR environments, thereby making our interface more accessible regardless of the hardware that is used.

Testing and Evaluation


In order to evaluate whether CAVECAD met our original specifications and goals, I designed and executed a user study with the help of my research colleagues.

Design and Overview

Duration

1 Hour (60 Minutes)
4 Tasks (15-20 mins each)
Post-task questionnaire (5-10 mins each)

Participants

10 Participants
Mean Age: 22
UCLA Students with no prior CAD experience

Design

Repeated measures, between-users design

The study required users to participate in pairs. Users were asked to perform 3 separate design tasks, each of which lasting between 15 to 20 minutes.

TASK 1: Participants designed 1 specified furniture with required parameters in the VR Environment.
TASK 2: Participants designed 1 specified furniture with required parameters in the AR Environment.
TASK 3: Participants designed 1 specified furniture in collaboration with another participant, one using AR and one using VR.
TASK 4: Same as task 3, but the devices were swtiched.

The furniture in each of the 4 tasks were different. However, among groups, each participant designed the same furnitures.

Data Collection and Analysis

All data was collected through questionnaires, which contained quantitative and qualitative measures for evaluating CAVECAD's ease of use, interactability, and efficiency when designing furniture in different scenarios. Additionally, subsequent videoed interviews were used to collect further verbal feedback from users.

The data was then analyzed using a recursive method akin to the Infinity Squares, where observations were iteratively categorized into common observed themes across all participants. A detailed analysis can be found in my research paper.
